


## Floods hit the famous village of Leavenworth, Wash., during its peak season, leaving businesses reeling and wondering if crowds will return LEAVENWORTH, Wash.

It' s no exaggeration to say this town runs on Christmas.

The alpine village, nestled in the Cascade mountains 120 miles east of Seattle, transforms each December into what Travel Leisure calls one of America’s “best Christmas towns.” A half million lights twinkle downtown. Tourists flock to reindeer farms. The local chamber bills the experience as “living inside a magical snow globe!”

This year, that snow globe shattered. Brutal December storms have pushed Leavenworth’s economy into uncharted territory during its normally busiest season. 

A week before Christmas, its downtown—home to dozens of Bavarian-style buildings that adhere to a strict design code—was unusually still. Streets ordinarily packed with tourists lay eerily quiet. Parking stalls, a notorious bottleneck in the town of fewer than 3,000, were open. Even the famed nutcracker museum sat largely empty.

Carolers meandered through town, drawing no more than a dozen onlookers as they sang “Angels We Have Heard on High” and asked shop owners whether holiday crowds might return.

“I hope so,” said one shopkeeper. “We all hope so.”

This was already shaping up to be a difficult year for a village reliant on tourism. In June, a manhunt for a military veteran accused of killing his three children closed a popular stretch of wilderness for about two weeks. In October, at the peak of Oktoberfest celebrations, a forest fire closed a major highway funneling visitors across the Cascades.

Then came the December storms. The winds arrived first. Dec. 8 was another busy Monday in a six-week stretch that typically brings many of the town’s estimated 3.4 million annual visitors. A walking-tour guide leaned against a guest to stay upright. Garbage bins tumbled down the street. Leaves and debris burst inside restaurants and up stairwells.

The Alpine Coaster at Leavenworth Adventure Park overlooks U.S. Highway 2, which has been closed following torrential rains this month.

Next arrived atmospheric rivers—intense weather systems that broke levees, flooded towns and killed at least one in Western Washington.

The storms dropped nearly 6 inches of rain in 10 days—more than the town typically sees all month—and melted mountain snowpack. Water crept into crawlspaces and living rooms and inundated the riverfront park. Rain saturated soil, bulged rivers and eroded hillsides.

Uphill from the swollen Wenatchee River, 62-year-old Lisa Hart sat in her recently deceased parents’ kitchen, watching friends drape a tarp over the roof. A tree had crashed through the house feet from where she had been sleeping. 

“Who wants to come over here and stay in town when it’s raining cats and dogs?” said Hart, a recently retired supermarket clerk, her hands still shaking a week later. 

The storms washed out a portion of U.S. Highway 2, the most direct route from greater Seattle, where nearly 60% of the money spent in Leavenworth originates—and severed access to a major ski resort. Washington’s governor has since announced that the mountain pass, one of the main routes across the Cascades, will remain closed for months.

The final blow: a three-day power outage. The “Village of Lights” and its roughly 500,000 bulbs went dark for a weekend.

At Bären Haus, a German restaurant downtown, Nicole Dunstan served a family of out-of-towners—among her few customers last week—who had spent four days in candlelight at a hotel. Some restaurants discarded thousands of dollars in fresh produce; others distributed perishables to staff or the community.

“I’ve never seen something like this,” Dunstan said. “It’s hurting all of us.”

Ponderosa pines towering more than 100 feet crashed into pens at Leavenworth Reindeer Farm, a popular destination outside town. A crew of about 25 employees spent three days chainsawing toppled trees and reconstructing fences.

Farm manager Erika Bowie estimated cleanup costs at about $75,000. The farm refunded $170,000 in tickets for three fully-booked days it was forced to close, she said.

Many employees had storm-damaged property but were anxious to return to the holiday rush. “They’re calling us asking, ‘When can we get back to work? We need the money,’” said Bowie.

Last year, according to the Leavenworth Chamber of Commerce, 14 of the city’s 20 busiest days by foot traffic were in December—a testament to why many call this a “Christmas capital.” This year, many question whether any December days will make the cut.

At Kris Kringl, a downtown shop where “it’s Christmas all year long,” one can find Christmas villages, Christmas-themed cuckoo clocks, Christmas-themed cookie jars, Christmas-themed table runners, “Deck the Halls” tea kettles and much more. Sales plummeted about 50% during and immediately after the storms, manager Ashley Ross estimated. Stickers on the floor indicating the winding path of the checkout line went unobscured; there was no line.

“That’s unheard of,” said Dayton Vesey, 30, surveying empty seating from behind the counter at Leavenworth Sausage Garten. 

Vesey typically makes $1,300 weekly during the holidays, he said. This year, that has dropped to about $500. A crew of nine serving bratwursts, kielbasas and beer steins had dwindled to one solitary staffer per shift.

Still, commercial life is reappearing. More than 850 people booked reindeer-farm tours the day the outpost reopened. Some hotels reported full occupancy for the weekend before Christmas. And the Squirrel Tree Resort—the first Bavarianized structure in the area—has offered free housing to those in need.

Meanwhile, the chamber is targeting its advertising in areas less affected by road closures, like Portland, Ore., Boise, Idaho, and Spokane, Wash. As the storm passes and water recedes, there’s a rallying cry up and down the main drag: Leavenworth is open for business.
People dining at Rheinhaus.

Others fear the worst has yet to come. The town typically sees a post-holidays slump, and some businesses have discussed reducing hours and staff or adopting higher-margin products. 

A new normal might not be so bad, said Florida transplant and local tour guide Coco Hopkins, invoking the town’s history.

In the 1950s, after the railroad departed and took the economy with it, two Seattleites conceived a plan to save the town: adopt a Bavarian theme and bet big on tourism.

“The reason we became the village that we are today is because we reinvented ourselves,” Hopkins said. 

“We can do that again. We’re that type of community.”
